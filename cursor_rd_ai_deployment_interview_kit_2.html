Role Overview

Why this role exists now

Cursor is scaling post-sales fast to meet enterprise demand. This leader owns the ADM team, sets the operating standard for how strategic customers deploy and adopt AI at scale, and ensures Cursor's post-sales motion can match the pace of the sales org. The company is mid-PLG-to-enterprise transition — this hire has to build durable systems while preserving the speed that makes Cursor dangerous.

What success looks like (6–12 months)

GRR trend is positive. A staffed, calibrated ADM team exists. Renewal forecasting is defensible. Strategic accounts have clear coverage plans. The ADM / FE / Sales handoff is clean and no longer ambiguous. Post-sales is a growth lever, not a support function.

What makes this hard at Cursor

Zero playbook to inherit. Developers and CTOs in the same account. Product ships weekly. PLG roots make customers resistant to structured engagement. Constant org ambiguity. Sales scaling fast creates coverage pressure. Enterprise expectations vs startup resourcing.

Key cross-functional dependencies

Sales (commercial alignment, expansion pipeline), Field Engineering (FE / FDE handoffs, technical depth layer), Product (roadmap advocacy, feedback loops), Marketing (customer stories, adoption narratives).

What this leader must build in year one

A staffed, high-bar ADM team. A renewal operating cadence that produces defensible forecasts. Clean ADM / FE / Sales handoffs. Strategic account plans for the top tier. And a post-sales motion that can scale with the sales org without adding friction or headcount linearly.

Peer Interview — Technical Acumen + First Principles

Take-Home Presentation — Leadership, Strategy + First Principles

Priya Bhatia + Seong 45 min (20 present / 25 Q&A) Presentation
Presentation Prompt (to be sent to candidate 48 hours in advance) Imagine you've just joined Cursor as Regional Director, AI Deployment. You've done your first two weeks of onboarding — you've talked to ADMs, Sales leaders, FEs, and a handful of strategic accounts. You're presenting to the Head of CS and a senior cross-functional leader. We want to hear how you think. Specifically:

(1) Diagnosis — Based on what you've seen, what are the two or three most important things post-sales needs to get right in the next 90 days? Where do you think the biggest risk is?

(2) Prioritization — Given limited resources, what do you actually do first — and what do you deliberately defer? Walk us through your reasoning.

(3) ADM Team Build — What does the right ADM look like for Cursor at this stage? What's the hiring bar, and how do you ramp them fast?

(4) Your Point of View — What's one thing you'd push for that might be controversial or different from how post-sales has traditionally been run?

Format: 15–20 slides max. We care about clarity of thinking more than deck polish. Come ready to defend every slide.
Q&A Probing Questions (use selectively)
DiagnosticYou said [X] is the biggest risk. What would have to be true for you to be wrong about that?
First PrinciplesYou said you'd defer [Y]. The Sales leader in this room is worried about [Y] too. Convince them to wait.
SituationalYour controversial take is [Z]. Priya pushes back: "That's not how we've done it — and here's why." How do you respond?
DiagnosticIf you could only hire two ADMs in the first 60 days, what profiles do you hire — and who do you say no to?
BehavioralWhat's the one slide in this deck you're least confident in — and what would it take to get conviction there?
What exceptional presentations look like
  • Diagnosis is opinionated — not "we need to do X, Y, and Z" but "the biggest risk is specifically this and here's why"
  • Prioritization is genuinely hard — they've said no to something important and explained the tradeoff honestly
  • ADM profile is specific and calibrated for Cursor's stage — not a generic "great communicator with technical background"
  • Controversial POV is actually controversial — not "we should be more customer-centric"
  • Q&A: holds the room under pushback — engages the challenge, doesn't cave immediately or dig in defensively
  • Deck is concise and tight — wastes no slides on preamble or bio
  • Can say "I don't know yet" on specific things, paired with "here's how I'd find out"
Red flags
  • Deck is beautiful but generic — could have been written about any CS org at any SaaS company
  • No real prioritization — everything is "first priority"
  • ADM profile is boilerplate: curious, customer-obsessed, technical — no specificity to Cursor's moment
  • Controversial POV is safe or derivative — no intellectual risk taken
  • Immediate capitulation under pushback — no conviction in their own analysis
  • Over-indexed on headcount requests or org charts before establishing the operating foundation
